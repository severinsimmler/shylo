Introducing

In 2002, John Burrows introduced a simple but surprisingly accurate method for authorship attribution, the so-called Delta procedure. Using relative frequencies of the most common words, it becomes possible to compare a set of texts and obtain sufficiently precise information on the most likely author of a text. Quoting Burrows’ original paper, “the object is to treat all of these [most common] words as markers of potentially equal power in highlighting the differences between one style and another” (Burrows 2002, 271).


About this application

Research on Delta, and authorship attribution in general, has become a popular topic in Digital Humanities. As a result, multiple variants of distance measures have been developed and evaluated (Evert et al. give a nice overview). Eight of them have been ported to a R package called stylo (Eder, Rybicki, Kestemont, 2016). Starting with low-level programming from inside the R console, stylo also provides a high-level GUI to produce static visualizations of the analysis (basically the results of statistical procedures on the distance scores, e.g. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and hierarchical clustering).

This application builds on top of the low-level functions of stylo, and empowers users to explore results of a stylometric analysis interactively, which is currently not possible with the package itself.
